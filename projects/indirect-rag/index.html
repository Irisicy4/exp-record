RAG-Accuracy Reward for Retriever Fine-Tuning

Does training a dense retriever on downstream RAG answer-accuracy beat standard relevance fine-tuning? Backbone Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B, LoRA. Evaluated on 5 datasets; ID=in training data, OOD=held out.

Each cell = full-corpus recall R@1·5·10·15 (×100) and acc = downstream RAG answer accuracy (cover-EM, fixed Qwen2.5-7B reader over top-5). Bright text = best R@5 in column. Mean = avg recall over the 5 datasets.
Cell fill = the cell's average recall vs zero-shot on that dataset: greener = better redder = worse (saturates at 6 pts).
Rows. base baselines: zero-shot and Tevatron (standard relevance recipe, InfoNCE+BM25 hard negs, no reader). ours anchored MNRL + precomputed reader guide, opt. WiSE-FT: vLogit = guide by reader logP(answer); vJudge = guide by k/8 cover-EM accuracy. online live RAG-acc reward (PPO, vLogit). Same training queries within a section.
anchor=1.0 marks runs trained with the L2-to-frozen-base anti-forgetting regularizer (all train_mnrl-based rows); anchor=0 = none (Tevatron). Comparisons are within-anchor-policy; zero-shot is the unmodified base model (same across all data mixes).

§ Takeaway

1 · Does our RAG-accuracy reward beat the base? Yes — once the learning rate is right.
• Weak base (bert): offline FT with the RAG-accuracy reward (vLogit/vJudge) beats the zero-shot base (any FT helps a headroom base), though it loses to relevance FT in every reward-swap cell.
• Strong base (Qwen3-Emb-0.6B): our v2 online RL landed below base — but the v3 sweep shows that was a learning-rate artifact, not the reward. At LR 1e-5 (10× lower, v3.1, everything else = our v2 recipe) our online RL beats base: HotpotQA L1 0.740 vs 0.711, L2 0.406 vs 0.382; 2Wiki L2 0.280 vs 0.242. At LR 1e-4 it collapses (v2-PPO 0.47), and dropping the anchors too at high LR is a total wipeout (R@5 0.00–0.08). Copying HARR's low temperature (0.05) actually hurts us; the one knob that mattered was the LR.
Bottom line: properly tuned (LR 1e-5), our RAG-accuracy online RL improves even a strong retriever — the earlier “collapse” was too-high LR. Whether it beats relevance SFT is the open question (see 4).
2 · HARR's gain is small but real and robust. Reproduced in HARR's own pipeline (wiki18 + Qwen3-8B ReAct, HotpotQA): the published adapter beats base by +0.6 EM query-only (paper +0.86), and +1.0 EM when the corpus is also re-encoded with the adapter (28.4 → 29.4). It does not collapse under both-sides re-encoding — so the gain is a genuinely better embedding, not a frozen-corpus artifact. Still modest (the paper calls it “modest but consistent”), and the no-stateful ablation is ≈ base — but real.

Key correction: both approaches are GRPO/PPO-style; ours actually adds KL and InfoNCE anti-collapse anchors and still drifts, while HARR uses neither. So robustness is not from regularizers — it comes from HARR's 10× lower LR (a near-frozen adapter). Confirmed by the v3 sweep: at LR 1e-5 our anchor-free run no longer collapses (v3.6), and our full recipe at LR 1e-5 (v3.1) beats base. The LR was the culprit, not the anchors or the reward.
4 · RAG-reward vs relevance SFT (matched data, both tuned): RAG-reward wins on downstream accuracy. Ran the clean head-to-head — relevance-SFT (MNRL / GISTEmbed, hard-neg pool, LR-swept) on the same 10k MuSiQue queries as v3.1, same base, same OOD eval. On L2 downstream accuracy v3.1 beats the best relevance-SFT on both datasets (2Wiki 0.280 vs 0.250, HotpotQA 0.406 vs 0.378); on L1 recall it is a tie (v3.1 wins HotpotQA 0.740 vs 0.731; relevance wins 2Wiki 0.659 vs 0.633). So the RAG-accuracy reward — which optimizes answer accuracy directly — wins exactly where it should (downstream QA) and matches relevance on raw recall. On equal data with both tuned: RAG-reward ≥ relevance SFT, strictly better on downstream accuracy.
5 · Weak base (bert): online RL works dramatically — but wants the OPPOSITE LR. bert-base is near-random zero-shot (2Wiki R@5 0.066, HotpotQA 0.227); our online RL lifts it to 0.534 / 0.544 — an 8× / 2.4× recall gain. Best LR is 1e-4 (high); 1e-5 barely learns (0.14/0.34). Symmetric to the strong base: a weak base has no geometry to preserve so it needs a high LR to learn; a strong base needs a low LR to avoid drifting off its pretrained geometry. So online RAG-accuracy RL is a real retriever-training method — huge gains on a weak base, modest gains on a strong one — with the LR tuned to the base.
The two “base” anchors are the same frozen model but measured in different pipelines/metrics; deltas are valid within each column, not across.

§ Training-data comparison — ours vs HARR

What each approach actually trains on. Two axes dominate: HARR trains on ~10× less data (2.4k vs our 10–40k) but retrieves over a ~1000× larger, realistic corpus (wiki18 21M vs our 12k) — so HARR's retriever gets a hard, realistic training signal, while our online RL over a 12k pool is near-trivial retrieval (little to learn). HARR is also in-domain (HotpotQA train & eval); our online RL is MuSiQue-trained, evaluated OOD.

§ HARR reproduction — authors' native pipeline (wiki18 21M + Qwen3-8B ReAct), HotpotQA

Reproducing HARR (“RFT for History-Aware Dense Retriever”) in its own eval: HARR's published Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B adapters, wiki18 (21M) retrieval, Qwen3-8B ReAct agent, SQuAD EM/F1 (n=500). HARR's real contribution is the history-aware retrieval state (its reward is answer-F1 only). Retrieval = exact GPU matmul over the same base-encoded corpus (Qdrant ingestion of 21M was unworkable on this cluster; applied identically to all rows). Verdict: reproduces — base EM matches (28.4 vs 28.74) and the gain matches the paper's own “modest but consistent” framing (full − base: ours +0.6 EM, paper +0.86 EM). F1 carries a uniform −2.5 offset (reader/answer-extraction config; base & adapters shift together, so the gain is preserved). Control (last row): re-encoding the corpus too with the adapter (not just the query) does not collapse recall — it lifts to +1.0 EM, so the adapter is a genuinely better encoder, unlike our online-RL runs which degrade the base.
